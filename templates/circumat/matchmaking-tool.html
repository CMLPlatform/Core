Introduction to the Industrial Symbiosis Matchmaking tool

The European Union has adopted an ambitious package to promote Circular Economy, aimed at fostering sustainable economic growth, generating new jobs and reducing environmental impacts. Part of the package establishes clear targets for waste reduction, including waste re-use and industrial symbiosis (IS). Industrial wastes need to be accounted and characterized in order to investigate their possible use as feedstock.

To establish an industrial symbiosis partnership, finding a partner firm for material exchange requires detailed data at company level. However, industrial waste data is often not available or may be difficult to obtain, either for confidentiality reasons or because it is time-consuming to collect. The task becomes even more challenging when the regional scale is considered, due to the large number of companies that need to be surveyed. On the other hand, application of IS at the regional scale has several advantages, including a greater variety of potential customers that may use the available resources, the provision of the volumes required to achieve economies of scale, and ensured proximity between companies.

The Industrial Symbiosis matchmaking tool is a framework that allows for a quick identification of potential re-uses of selected wastes given the characteristics of the region that is being studied, and the priorities practitioners have.

The tool for identifying scenarios for industrial waste utilization is intended to be flexible and possible to adapt to the development priorities established for a given region. The tool includes 3 main components: the selection of development priorities to focus on, waste selection and characterization, and the industrial symbiosis identification. The development priorities are based on the needs and opportunities of the studied region. These priorities determine which waste types are to be studied. The waste selection and characterization is made up of 4 steps. In Step 1, a list is produced, where the specified waste types are expressed in terms of waste categories within the standard waste nomenclatures. In Step 2, a characterization of wastes produced in the different economic sectors is made, using waste generation statistics and calculation of waste generation profiles by sector. Sectors that produce the wastes included in the list from Step 1 are selected for further analysis. In Step 3, detailed characterization of the waste generation of each industry within the selected sectors is carried out, allowing a refined selection of industries. Finally, in step 4, the expected quantities of waste produced by the industries selected in Step 3 are estimated, based on calculations of the waste intensity for each industry.
